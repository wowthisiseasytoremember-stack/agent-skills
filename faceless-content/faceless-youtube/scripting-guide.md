# YouTube Script Writing Guide

## Core Philosophy

A faceless YouTube video lives or dies on its script. Without a face to build parasocial connection, the WORDS are doing all the work. Every sentence must either inform, intrigue, or entertain. Ideally two of three.

---

## The Hook (First 30 Seconds)

YouTube's algorithm weighs the first 30 seconds more heavily than any other part of the video. If viewers click away here, the video is dead regardless of how good the rest is.

### Hook Formulas That Work

**The Contradiction Hook**
"Everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong. And the real answer is way more interesting."

**The Stakes Hook**
"In 2019, a single decision cost [entity] $4.7 billion. Here's what happened."

**The Promise Hook**
"By the end of this video, you'll understand [valuable thing] better than 99% of people."

**The Question Hook**
"What would happen if [provocative scenario]? Scientists actually tested this."

**The Story Hook**
"On March 15th, 1998, a man walked into [place] with nothing but [object]. Six months later, he [incredible outcome]."

### Hook Rules
- Never start with "Hey guys" or "Welcome to my channel." Immediate scroll.
- Never start with the video title restated. They already read it.
- First sentence = pattern interrupt. Say something unexpected.
- Create an information gap in the first 10 seconds that takes 8+ minutes to close.
- Specificity sells. "$4.7 billion" hits harder than "billions of dollars."

## Script Pacing

### Sentence Length Rhythm
Monotonous pacing kills retention. Vary sentence length deliberately:

"The company was founded in 1987. Nothing special about it. Just another startup in a sea of startups, doing what startups do — burning cash and making promises. But then something happened. Something nobody predicted. In a single quarter, they went from $2 million in revenue to $47 million. And the way they did it changed an entire industry forever."

Notice the rhythm: short, short, long, short, short, long, medium. This creates a pulse that keeps the listener engaged.

### The "Curiosity Refresh" Technique
Every 60-90 seconds, plant a new open loop:
- "But that's not even the craziest part."
- "And this is where it gets really interesting."
- "Now here's what nobody talks about."
- "But there's a catch."

These micro-hooks prevent the mid-video drop-off that kills average view duration.

### Transitions Between Points
Don't use "Moving on to point number two." Instead:
- "But [previous point] only tells half the story."
- "And that brings us to the part that surprised even the experts."
- "Now you might be thinking — okay, but what about [natural objection]?"

## Writing for the Ear

### Words to Avoid
YouTube scripts are SPOKEN, not read. Avoid:
- "Furthermore" → "And here's the thing"
- "Nevertheless" → "But"
- "Approximately" → "About" or "Around"
- "Subsequently" → "Then" or "After that"
- "Utilize" → "Use"
- "In order to" → "To"
- "It is important to note that" → Cut entirely

### Contractions Are Mandatory
- "It is" → "It's"
- "They are" → "They're"
- "Would not" → "Wouldn't"

Reading a script without contractions sounds robotic, especially with TTS voices.

### The "Say It Out Loud" Test
Before finalizing, read the script aloud (or run it through TTS). If any sentence makes you stumble, rewrite it. The mouth knows what the brain doesn't.

## Niche-Specific Script Templates

### Template: Explainer / "How X Works"
```
[HOOK: Surprising fact or misconception about X]
[CONTEXT: Why understanding X matters to the viewer]
[SIMPLE EXPLANATION: X explained at the highest level, using an analogy]
[DEEPER LAYER 1: First level of complexity, building on the analogy]
[DEEPER LAYER 2: Second level — this is where most viewers learn something new]
[DEEPER LAYER 3: Expert-level insight — "what most people get wrong"]
[IMPLICATIONS: What this means for the viewer's life/decisions]
[CTA: Comment prompt related to the topic]
```

### Template: Listicle / "Top N"
```
[HOOK: Why this list matters / what's at stake]
[HONORABLE MENTIONS: 1-2 quick ones that didn't make the cut (builds credibility)]
[ITEM N: Start from least impressive]
  - What it is (1-2 sentences)
  - Why it's on the list (the interesting part — spend 70% of time here)
  - One surprising detail
[... repeat, ascending in impressiveness ...]
[ITEM 1: The most impressive / surprising]
  - Extended treatment, 2x the length of others
[CTA: "Which one surprised you most? Comment below."]
```

### Template: Story / Narrative
```
[HOOK: The most dramatic moment, pulled forward in time]
[REWIND: "But to understand how we got here, we need to go back to..."]
[ACT 1 - SETUP: Characters, setting, initial situation]
[INCITING INCIDENT: What changed / what went wrong]
[ACT 2 - RISING ACTION: Complications, attempts, failures]
[TURNING POINT: The key decision or discovery]
[ACT 3 - CLIMAX: The dramatic peak]
[RESOLUTION: What happened after]
[LESSON / REFLECTION: What this means, why it matters]
[CTA]
```

### Template: Comparison / "X vs Y"
```
[HOOK: Common assumption about X vs Y that's wrong]
[CONTEXT: Why this comparison matters]
[CRITERION 1: Compare X vs Y on first metric]
[CRITERION 2: Compare on second metric]
[CRITERION 3: Compare on third metric]
[THE NUANCE: "But here's what most comparisons miss..."]
[VERDICT: Clear recommendation with caveats]
[CTA: "Which one do you use? Let me know."]
```

## Visual Prompt Writing

The `[VISUAL: ...]` tags in scripts serve double duty — they guide video editors AND can be used as prompts for AI image/video generation.

### Good Visual Prompts
- Specific: `[VISUAL: Aerial shot of Manhattan skyline at sunset, camera slowly panning left to right]`
- Mood-aware: `[VISUAL: Dark, moody close-up of circuit boards with blue LED reflections, slow zoom in]`
- Action-oriented: `[VISUAL: Timelapse of stock market ticker showing rapid price increases, green numbers]`

### Bad Visual Prompts
- Vague: `[VISUAL: Something techy]`
- Unfilmable: `[VISUAL: The concept of freedom]`
- Too complex: `[VISUAL: Split screen showing 6 different charts while a person walks through a door]`

### Visual Pacing Rules
- Change the visual every 5-8 seconds. Longer than 10 seconds on one image = viewer boredom.
- Match visual energy to script energy. Dramatic moment = dynamic visuals. Calm explanation = steady, clean visuals.
- B-roll should SUPPORT the narration, not compete with it. If the visual is too interesting, viewers stop listening.

| 5 minutes       | 750                           | 850                                  | 950                           |

| 10 minutes      | 1,500                         | 1,700                                | 1,900                         |
| 12 minutes      | 1,800                         | 2,040                                | 2,280                         |
| 15 minutes      | 2,250                         | 2,550                                | 2,850                         |

**Sweet spot for monetization**: 8-12 minutes. Long enough for mid-roll ads, short enough to maintain retention. YouTube now allows mid-rolls on 8+ minute videos (changed from 10 minutes in 2023).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Starting with context instead of hook.** Nobody cares about background until you make them care.
2. **Being comprehensive instead of interesting.** Cut 30% of your facts. Keep the ones that make people say "wait, really?"
3. **Neutral tone throughout.** Vary between confident statements, rhetorical questions, and moments of surprise.
4. **No clear structure.** Viewers should always know roughly where they are in the video's arc.
5. **Ending with "Thanks for watching."** End with a strong final thought or call to action. "Thanks for watching" is dead air.
6. **Writing for readers instead of listeners.** If a sentence has a semicolon, rewrite it as two sentences.
